Welcome!

This tool teaches you how computers learn to speak using a technique called limited-domain speech synthesis.

What is this tool?

You are going to build a working computer voice — one that sounds like you. By the end, you will be able to type a new airport announcement and hear it read back in your own voice.

You do not need any programming experience. Everything happens right here in your browser. Your recordings are stored only on your device and are never sent to the internet.

What is "limited-domain speech synthesis"?

Speech synthesis means making a computer talk in a human-sounding voice. A limited-domain system is one that can only talk about one specific topic — in our case, airport announcements.

Because we know exactly what kinds of phrases an airport announcement will contain, we can record just those phrases and combine them to make new announcements. This is much simpler than building a general-purpose voice that can say anything, and it is how early automated announcement systems (train stations, airports, telephone services) were built.

The underlying technique is called unit selection synthesis: the computer picks ("selects") the right recorded "units" (short phrases) and joins them together to produce new speech.

How does this tool work?

  1. The Corpus (Step 2): You will see the complete list of phrases you need to record. These phrases are called the corpus — the raw material of your voice database.
  2. Recording (Step 3): You record yourself saying each phrase. Your browser microphone is used; no special equipment is needed.
  3. Voice Database (Step 4): Your recordings are organised into a voice database — a searchable library of speech units.
  4. Synthesis (Step 5): You type any airport announcement and the system picks the right units from your database, joins them together, and plays the result back in your voice.

Step 1 · The Recording Script (Corpus)

Before recording a single word, speech engineers design exactly what to record. This plan is called the corpus.

What is a corpus?

A corpus (plural: corpora) is a carefully chosen collection of sentences or phrases that a voice actor records. Think of it like a script for a play — except instead of performing it once, the recordings become a permanent library that the computer uses to build new speech.

The key challenge for the corpus designer is coverage: making sure every word or phrase that might ever appear in a new announcement is already in the database. If the user later types a city that was never recorded, the system will not be able to say it.

How was this corpus designed?

Our corpus is organised into speech units — short phrases, each covering a specific part of an airport announcement:

  • Opening phrases — how to start an announcement
  • Flight numbers — spoken digit-by-digit (aviation style)
  • Destinations — one phrase per city
  • Flight status — boarding, delayed, cancelled, etc.
  • Gate numbers — one phrase per gate
  • Delay durations — 30 minutes, 1 hour, etc.
  • Instructions — directions for passengers
  • Closing phrases — polite endings

When the system synthesizes a new announcement, it simply picks the right unit from each category and plays them one after another. This is the essence of unit selection synthesis.

💡 Why say flight numbers digit by digit? In real aviation, flight numbers are always spoken as individual digits — for example, "Flight one zero one" rather than "Flight one hundred and one". This removes any ambiguity about whether "101" means one-oh-one or a hundred-and-one.

What happens in a professional system?

In a professional voice-building workflow (such as the one described in the FestVox documentation), the corpus is carefully chosen to cover all the required phones (individual speech sounds) in all the contexts in which they can appear. The recording is done in a sound-proof studio, and special software then automatically aligns the transcript to the audio to find the precise start and end time of every phone.

Our system is simpler: instead of individual phones we use whole phrases as units, and instead of automatic alignment we use your natural phrase recordings directly. This makes the quality lower (you may hear small "jumps" at the joins) but makes the process easy to understand and try out.

Step 2 · Record Your Voice

How does recording work?

When you click Record, your browser will ask permission to use your microphone (if it hasn't already). Say the phrase shown, then click Stop. You can play back the recording immediately and re-record if you are not happy with it.

In professional speech synthesis, recordings are made in a quiet studio with a high-quality microphone to minimise background noise. For this exercise, any quiet room and a standard laptop or phone microphone will work fine.

🎙 Tips for good recordings
  • Find a quiet room and reduce background noise (fans, traffic, etc.).
  • Speak clearly and at a natural pace — as if making a real announcement.
  • Keep a consistent distance from the microphone (about 20–30 cm).
  • Speak each phrase naturally, including brief silence before and after.
  • If you stumble, just stop and re-record — it takes only a moment.

Step 3 · Your Voice Database

Your recordings are now organised into a voice database — a searchable library of speech units.

What is a voice database?

A voice database (sometimes called a speech corpus or unit inventory) is the set of all the recorded speech units available for synthesis. Think of it like a well-organised filing cabinet: each drawer is a category (opening phrases, flight numbers, destinations, …) and each folder inside holds one recording.

When the synthesis system needs to say something, it opens the right drawer, picks the right folder, and plays the recording.

What does a professional voice database look like?

In a full unit-selection system (like Festival, MaryTTS, or the systems described in the FestVox guide), the database stores audio at the level of individual phones (the smallest speech sounds, such as the sounds in "cat": /k/, /æ/, /t/). Each phone may have many different recordings taken from different positions in words, giving the selection algorithm more choices and allowing smoother joins between units.

Our database stores whole phrases. This is simpler, and for a limited domain (airport announcements) it works surprisingly well.

How does unit selection synthesis work?

When you click Synthesize, the system runs through these steps:

  1. Text normalisation: Your text is converted to lower case, numbers are expanded to words (e.g. "101" → "one zero one", "gate 5" → "gate five"), and punctuation is removed.
  2. Unit selection: The system scans your text from left to right, always trying to match the longest phrase it can find in the voice database (this is called greedy longest-match). For example, it will prefer "attention all passengers" over just "attention".
  3. Audio loading: The recordings for the selected units are loaded from your browser's storage.
  4. Concatenation: The audio clips are joined end-to-end with a short silence gap between each unit.
  5. Playback: The combined audio is played through your speakers.

The visualisation below the synthesize button shows exactly which units were selected (in blue) and which words could not be matched because they were never recorded (in red).

How does a professional system handle joins?

In our simple system the joins between units can sometimes sound a little abrupt or unnatural. A professional unit-selection synthesizer such as Festival or a modern concatenative TTS engine improves this by:

  • Storing many alternative recordings of each unit (from different positions in longer sentences) and choosing the pair of adjacent units that join most smoothly.
  • Using a join cost function — a mathematical measure of how well two units connect — to guide the selection.
  • Applying signal processing at the join points to smooth the transition.

In our simplified system we use a single recording per unit and just place an 80-millisecond gap between them. You will often be able to hear where each unit ends and the next begins — which actually makes the underlying mechanism very clear!
