Why record full sentences — not individual words?

Human speech sounds natural only when words are spoken in context. The same word sounds different at the start of a sentence, in the middle, and at the end — because your pitch, rhythm, and phrasing all change depending on what comes next. Isolated word recordings produce choppy, robotic output.

By recording complete, natural sentences and then extracting the individual phrase units afterwards, we preserve that natural prosody even in the extracted units. This is the approach used by real airport TTS systems.

How does the extraction work?

After you record each sentence, the system runs a process called Voice Activity Detection (VAD): it measures the energy (loudness) of the audio moment by moment and finds the natural pauses between phrases. Those pause points become the boundaries between phrase units.

If the automatic detection is not confident (because the room was noisy or speech was very fast), the system falls back to splitting the audio proportionally based on how long each phrase is in the text. Professional tools instead use forced alignment — software that compares a phoneme-level model of the sentence against the audio to find exact boundaries — but that requires a server. Our browser-based approach is simpler and good enough for learning.

Step 1 · The Recording Script (Corpus)

Before recording anything, professional voice engineers design a carefully chosen set of sentences — the corpus.

What is a corpus?

A corpus is the complete set of sentences a voice actor records for a speech synthesis system. Every word, phrase, and sound that might ever appear in a new announcement must be present somewhere in the corpus. If a word was never recorded, the synthesizer cannot say it.

Good corpus design means choosing the smallest possible set of sentences that still covers every required unit. Adding unnecessary sentences wastes recording time and storage; missing a single phrase makes the synthesizer fall silent for that word.

How is each sentence split into phrase units?

Each sentence in our corpus has been pre-annotated with the list of phrase units it contains — for example, “Attention all passengers, flight one zero one to New York is now boarding at gate five.” contains the units:

  • “Attention all passengers”
  • “flight one zero one”
  • “to New York”
  • “is now boarding”
  • “at gate five”

When you record the sentence, the system uses silence detection (VAD) to find the pauses between these phrases and cut the audio at those points — no server needed.

What does a professional system do differently?

Tools like FestVox use forced alignment: a Hidden Markov Model is trained on the language, then run against the audio to find the exact start and end time of every individual phoneme (speech sound). This gives boundaries accurate to a few milliseconds. Our browser-based silence detector is less precise (~100–200 ms accuracy), but the concept is the same.

💡 Aviation digit-by-digit convention Flight numbers are spoken as individual digits ("one zero one", not "one hundred and one") to remove ambiguity. When you type "Flight 101" in the synthesis step, the system automatically expands it to "flight one zero one" before matching.

Step 2 · Record Your Voice

Say each complete sentence naturally — as if you were a real airport announcer. The system will automatically extract the individual phrase units from your recording.

What happens after you record?

When you click Stop, your recording is saved and the system runs Voice Activity Detection in the background: it finds the energy level in short 20-millisecond windows and looks for pauses (silence) longer than 80 ms. Each long pause becomes the boundary between two phrase units.

You can click Play back to hear your recording. If it sounds wrong — you stumbled, there was background noise, or you paused in the wrong place — just click Re-record.

🎙 Tips for good recordings
  • Find a quiet room with no fans, traffic, or air conditioning.
  • Speak clearly at a natural, steady pace — not too slow.
  • Keep your mouth about 20–30 cm from the microphone.
  • Say the whole sentence in one continuous recording.
  • Leave a brief natural pause at each comma — this helps the silence detector.

Step 3 · Your Voice Database

Your recordings form a voice database — the library the synthesizer will search when building new announcements.

What is stored?

For each sentence you recorded, the database stores the full audio and the pre-defined phrase unit boundaries. During synthesis, when the system needs, say, "to new york", it finds the sentence that contains the unit dest-newyork, loads the audio, and uses the Segmenter to extract just that slice.

In a production system this library would instead store individual phoneme recordings, each tagged with acoustic features (fundamental frequency, spectral envelope, duration) so the selection algorithm can minimise the acoustic discontinuity at every join. Our phrase-level approach is simpler but teaches the same concept.

Step 4 · Synthesize a New Announcement

Type any airport announcement and hear it played back in your own voice.

What happens when you click Synthesize?

  1. Text normalisation: Your text is lower-cased, numbers are expanded ("101" → "one zero one", "gate 5" → "gate five"), and punctuation is removed.
  2. Unit selection (greedy longest-match): The system scans your text from left to right. At each position it tries to match the longest phrase unit that exists in your database. For example, it prefers "attention all passengers" over just "attention".
  3. Segment extraction: For each selected unit, the Segmenter loads the sentence recording that contains it and cuts out the phrase slice using the pre-computed boundaries.
  4. Concatenation: The extracted audio slices are joined end-to-end with an 80 ms silence gap between each one.
  5. Playback: The combined audio — in your voice — is played through your speakers.

The visualisation below the Synthesize button shows every phrase unit that was selected (in blue) and any words the system could not find in your database (in red — those words are silently skipped).

Why might joins sound unnatural?

Two phrases recorded in different sentences were spoken in different prosodic contexts — different pitch, different speed, different volume at the end. When joined back-to-back these mismatches are audible.

A production concatenative synthesizer minimises this with a join cost function: it picks the pair of candidate units that is acoustically most similar at the join point (same fundamental frequency and spectral shape), then applies PSOLA signal processing to smooth the transition. Our system just places an 80 ms gap — which actually makes the mechanism very transparent for learning!
